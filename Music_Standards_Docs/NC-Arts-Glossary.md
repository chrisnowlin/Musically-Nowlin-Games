## Arts Education General Glossary

> **Note:** These glossary terms are found in multiple Standard Courses of Study and are meaningful to teachers of all Arts Education disciplines.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| [[19th Century]] | A century is a period of a hundred years. For example, the 19th century was the period from 1801 to 1900.[^5] |
| [[Accompaniment]] | See *Musical Accompaniment*. |
| [[Adapt]] | To change, or to change something, to suit different conditions or uses.[^4] |
| [[Analyze]] | Identifying and examining separate parts as they function independently and together in creative works and study of the arts.[^22] |
| [[Appropriate]] | Suitable or right for a particular situation or occasion.[^4] |
| [[Appropriation]] | Intentional borrowing, copying, and alteration of pre-existing images and objects.[^17] |
| [[Artist]] | A person who creates art (such as painting, sculpture, music, or writing) using conscious skill and creative imagination; a person skilled in any of the arts.[^14] |
| [[Artistic Intent]] | The purpose, main idea, and expressive or communicative goal(s) of a composition, study, work, or performance.[^17] |
| [[Audience]] | A reading, viewing, or listening public.[^14] |
| [[Characteristics]] | Attribute, feature, property, or essential quality (of form/structure). Terms drawn from traditional, modern, and contemporary sources that identify the range of attributes used to describe works of art and design.[^17] |
| [[Cite]] | To quote by way of example, authority, or proof.[^14] |
| [[Civilization]] | The culture characteristic of a particular time or place.[^14] |
| [[Collaboratively]] | Joining with others in attentive participation in an activity of imagining, exploring, and/or making.[^17] |
| [[Community]] | A unified body of individuals with a common characteristic or interest living together within a larger society.[^14] |
| [[Connect]] | Relationship among artistic ideas, personal meaning, and/or external context.[^17] |
| [[Context]] | Interrelated conditions surrounding the creation and experiencing of an artwork, including the artist, viewer/audiences, time, culture, presentation, and location.[^17] |
| [[Copyright]] | Form of protection grounded in the U.S. Constitution and granted by law for original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression.[^17] |
| [[Create]] | Conceiving and developing new artistic ideas and work.[^17] |
| [[Creative Commons]] | Copyright-license templates that provide a simple, standardized way to give the public permission to share and use creative work on conditions of the maker's choice.[^6] |
| [[Criteria]] | The rules or guidelines used for categorizing or judging; in arts assessment, the rules used to judge the quality of a student's performance.[^28] |
| [[Critique]] | Individual or collective reflective process by which artists or designers experience, analyze, and evaluate work(s) of art or design.[^17] |
| [[Cross-Curricular]] | Relating to or involving different courses offered by a school.[^14] |
| [[Cultural Context]] | Ideas, beliefs, values, norms, customs, traits, practices, and characteristics shared by individuals within a group that form the circumstances surrounding the creation, presentation, preservation, and response to art.[^17] |
| [[Culture]] | Values and beliefs of a particular group of people, from a specific place or time, expressed through characteristics such as tradition, social structure, religion, art, and food.[^17] |
| [[Customs]] | A usage or practice common to many or to a particular place or class; the whole body of usages, practices, or conventions that regulate social life.[^14] |
| [[Describe]] | To give a written or spoken report of how something is done or of what someone or something is like.[^4] |
| [[Develop]] | To grow or change into a more advanced, larger, or stronger form; to invent something or bring something into existence.[^4] |
| [[Digital Tools and Media]] | Anything in electronic form including photos, images, video, audio files, or artwork created or presented through electronic means.[^17] |
| [[Diverse]] | Including many different types of people or things.[^4] |
| [[Domains]] | An area (or range) over which someone or something acts, exists, or has influence or significance.[^14] |
| [[Embody]] | To physicalize a movement, concept, or idea through the body.[^17] |
| [[Emerging]] | Newly formed or prominent.[^14] |
| [[Ethical]] | Moral guidelines and philosophical principles for determining appropriate behavior.[^17] |
| [[Etiquette]] | Social behavior observed by those attending performances, which can vary depending upon the type of performance.[^17] |
| [[Evaluate]] | A judgment about the worth or quality of something. In education, data from tests, tasks, or performances are used to make judgments about the success of the student or program.[^28] |
| [[Evoke]] | To make someone remember something or feel an emotion.[^4] |
| [[Exemplify]] | To show or illustrate by example; to be an instance of or serve as an example.[^14] |
| [[Explain]] | To make something clear or easy to understand by describing or giving information about it.[^4] |
| [[Explore]] | Investigate multiple possibilities to learn more about an idea.[^17] |
| [[Fair Use]] | Limitation in copyright law which sets out factors to be considered in determining whether a particular use of one's work is "fair."[^17] |
| [[Fairness]] | Complying with appropriate, ethical, and equitable rules and guidelines.[^17] |
| [[Feedback]] | A reaction or response to a particular design problem or design solution.[^29] |
| [[Formal]] | Following or according with established form, custom, or rule.[^14] |
| [[Genre]] | Category of art or design identified by similarities in form, subject matter, content, or technique.[^17] |
| [[Given]] | Qualities or traits for assessing achievement level that are provided to students.[^17] |
| [[Guidance]] | Assistance provided temporarily to enable a student to perform a task that would be difficult to perform unaided.[^17] |
| [[Heritage]] | Something transmitted by or acquired from a predecessor; tradition.[^14] |
| [[Ideas]] | An entity (such as a thought, concept, sensation, or image) actually or potentially present to consciousness.[^14] |
| [[Identify]] | To recognize someone or something and be able to say who or what they are.[^27] |
| [[Improvise]] | To make, invent, or arrange offhand; to make or fabricate out of what is conveniently on hand.[^14] |
| [[Independently]] | Working with virtually no assistance, initiating appropriate requests for consultation, performing in a self-directed manner.[^17] |
| [[Indigenous]] | Of or relating to the earliest known inhabitants of a place, especially one that was colonized by a now-dominant group.[^14] |
| [[Infer]] | A thinking skill which enables one to go beyond available information to identify what reasonably may be true.[^19] |
| [[Informal]] | Not following or according with established form, custom, or rule.[^14] |
| [[Innovations]] | Imagining and conceiving something new and unexpected, including fresh ideas and ways of looking at things.[^17] |
| [[Interpret]] | To explain or tell the meaning of; to represent by means of art.[^14] |
| [[Justify]] | To prove or show to be just, right, or reasonable.[^14] |
| [[Legal]] | The legislated parameters and protocols of media arts systems, including user agreements, publicity releases, copyright, etc.[^17] |
| [[Marginalized Groups]] | Relegated to a marginal position within a society or group; excluded from or existing outside the mainstream.[^14] |
| [[Media]] | The various commercial and informational channels and forums for media artworks (TV, radio, internet, fine arts, non-profit, communications, etc.).[^29] |
| [[Musical Accompaniment]] | Auditory sounds that accompany a dance work (may or may not be music); can refer to music, voice, soundscapes (both natural and man-made).[^1] |
| [[Open Source]] | Computer software for which the copyright holder freely provides the right to use, study, change, and distribute the software to anyone for any purpose.[^26] |
| [[Original (Dance / Music / Theatre / Visual Artwork)]] | Not secondary, derivative, or imitative; being the first instance or source from which a copy, reproduction, or translation is or can be made.[^14] |
| [[Parody]] | A literary or musical work in which the style of an author or work is closely imitated for comic effect or in ridicule.[^14] |
| [[Perform]] | Process of realizing artistic ideas and work through interpretation and presentation.[^17] |
| [[Performance]] | Experience of engaging in the act of presenting artistic skills in a classroom or private or public venue.[^17] |
| [[Personal]] | Unique experiences and relationships that surround a single person, influenced by personal life, family, habits, interests, and/or preferences.[^17] |
| [[Post-Secondary]] | Of or relating to education beyond high school.[^7] |
| [[Present]] | Share artistic work (e.g., a composition) with others.[^17] |
| [[Presentation]] | An activity in which an individual or team shows, describes, or explains a design solution to a group of people.[^29] |
| [[Production Elements]] | Aspects of performance that produce theatrical effects (costumes, makeup, sound, lighting, props).[^17] |
| [[Professional]] | Participating for gain or livelihood in an activity or field of endeavor; having a particular profession as a permanent career.[^14] |
| [[Public Domain]] | A work of authorship no longer under copyright protection, or which failed to meet the requirements for copyright protection. Works in the public domain may be used freely.[^32] |
| [[Refine]] | Make changes in works or performances to more effectively realize intent through technical quality or expression.[^17] |
| [[Region]] | Relating to or coming from a particular part of a country.[^4] |
| [[Relate]] | To show or establish logical or causal connection between.[^14] |
| [[Respond]] | Understanding and evaluating how the arts convey meaning.[^17] |
| [[Select]] | To choose a small number of things, or to choose by making careful decisions.[^4] |
| [[Society / Societal]] | An enduring and cooperating social group whose members have developed organized patterns of relationships through interaction with one another.[^14] |
| [[Styles]] | Specific movement characteristics, qualities, or principles that give distinctive identity; may also refer to the unique artistic choices of a particular artist.[^17] |
| [[Support]] | See *Guidance*. |
| [[Synthesize]] | Combining or integrating information.[^19] |
| [[Technique]] | Specific skills, pedagogies, theories, or methods of investigation used by an artist.[^17] |
| [[Technology]] | The mechanical aspects and contexts of arts production, including hardware, software, networks, code, etc.[^17] |
| [[Theme]] | A subject or topic of discourse or of artistic representation.[^14] |
| [[Traditional]] | Following or conforming to tradition; adhering to past practices or established conventions.[^14] |
| [[Traditions]] | Pattern of practices and beliefs within a societal group.[^17] |
| [[Values]] | Relative worth, utility, or importance.[^14] |
| [[Vocational]] | The workforce aspects and contexts of media arts.[^17] |
| [[Works]] | Something produced by the exercise of creative talent or expenditure of creative effort; artistic production.[^14] |

## Dance Glossary

> **Note:** These terms are found in addition to those in the General Glossary, in the K-12 Dance Standard Course of Study.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| [[Abstract Movement / Abstraction]] | To remove movement from a particular or representative context and, by manipulating it through the elements of space, time, and energy, create a new sequence or dance that retains the essence of the original.[^18] |
| [[Alignment]] | The process of adjusting the skeletal and muscular system to gravity to support effective functionality.[^17] |
| [[Basic Choreographic Forms]] | The organization of choreography and movement to fulfill artistic intent of a dance or dance study (e.g., AB, ABA, theme and variation).[^2] |
| [[Body Awareness]] | Paying attention to and being aware of internal bodily sensations.[^13] |
| [[Body Parts]] | Early grades focus on head, back, arms, legs, hands, and feet. As the dancer progresses, more emphasis is placed on the joints (wrists, elbows, shoulders, ankles, knees, hip sockets, spine, jaw, fingers, toes) and specific muscles, bones, organs, etc.[^11] |
| [[Body Shapes]] | The spatial contour the body makes: curved, angular, twisted, straight, symmetrical, or asymmetrical.[^18] |
| [[Choreographic Forms]] | See *Basic Choreographic Forms*.[^2] |
| [[Choreographic Intent]] | The purpose, main idea, and expressive or communicative goal(s) of a dance composition study, work, or performance.[^17] |
| [[Choreographic Principles]] | Compositional elements in dance; factors to be considered to attain aesthetically satisfying dance composition (intent, form/design, theme, repetition).[^31] |
| [[Choreographic Processes]] | Techniques that assist choreographers in developing dance works by deliberate choice making, reflection upon the work-in-progress, and exploration and planning in reference to a specific artistic intention.[^29] |
| [[Clarity of Movement]] | The ability to execute each step in a dance clearly and completely.[^12] |
| [[Coordination]] | The harmonious functioning of parts for effective results.[^14] |
| [[Cue]] | A signal (word, phrase, or bit of stage business) to a performer to begin a specific speech or action.[^14] |
| [[Dance Elements]] | The key components of movement: body using space, time, and energy; often referred to as the elements of movement.[^17] |
| [[Dance Phrases]] | A sequence of movements ordered based upon a preconceived choreographic intent with a clear beginning, middle, and end.[^29] |
| [[Dance Sequence]] | A brief sequence of movements that communicates artistic intent; may contain as few as two or three movements.[^29] |
| [[Dance Work]] | A complete dance that has a beginning, middle (development), and end.[^17] |
| [[Depict]] | To represent or show something.[^4] |
| [[Directions]] | A movement element in dance: forward, backward, sideward, diagonally, circularly, up, or down.[^29] |
| [[Duet]] | A dance performed by two people.[^3] |
| [[Dynamics of Movement]] | The qualities or characteristics of movement which lend expression and style; also called "efforts" or "energy" (e.g., lyrical, sustained, quick, light, strong).[^23] |
| [[Element of Dance]] | See *Dance Elements*. |
| [[Endurance]] | The ability to make a lot of physical effort over a long period of time.[^4] |
| [[Flexibility]] | The ability of muscles, joints, and soft tissues to move through an unrestricted, pain-free range of motion.[^10] |
| [[Focus]] | Conscious attention toward a certain point; with eyes, body parts, or direction. Involves the whole body to communicate the intention of the dance.[^11] |
| [[Foundational Dance Skills]] | Fundamental dance skills (alignment, coordination, balance, core support, kinesthetic awareness).[^17] |
| [[Historical Period]] | When the dance was made; relevant developments in that era may influence the dance.[^11] |
| [[Improvisation]] | Movement created spontaneously, occurring within free or highly structured environments but always with an element of chance.[^18] |
| [[Infer]] | To derive as a conclusion from facts or premises.[^14] |
| [[Kinesthetic Awareness]] | Pertaining to sensations and understanding of bodily movement.[^17] |
| [[Kinesthetic Empathy]] | The capacity to participate in somebody's movement or their sensory experience of movement; uses mirroring of sensations to enhance connection and synchronicity between people.[^30] |
| [[Levels of Movement]] | The altitude of a movement or shape (high, middle, low) in relationship to its distance from the floor.[^29] |
| [[Motor Skills]] | A function that involves specific movements of the body's muscles to perform tasks (walking, running, swimming).[^15] |
| [[Movement Qualities]] | The essential nature and quantity of energy expended in a movement; subtle variations in treatment of movement contrasts. Sometimes referred to as dynamics or efforts.[^29] |
| [[Movement Score]] | A written notation of a musical or dance composition, allowing the work to be performed later or by another performer.[^16] |
| [[Muscular Strength]] | One of the accepted components of total fitness, which includes endurance, flexibility, power, and speed.[^9] |
| [[Musical Cues]] | See *Cue*. |
| [[Pattern]] | A set phrase of music or movement that can then be repeated.[^11] |
| [[Production Element]] | Aspects of performance that produce theatrical effects (costumes, makeup, sound, lighting, props).[^17] |
| [[Production Terminology]] | Words commonly used to refer to the stage, performance setting, or theatrical aspects of dance presentation.[^17] |
| [[Projection]] | A confident presentation of one's body and energy to communicate movement and meaning vividly to an audience; also refers to performance quality.[^29] |
| [[Prop]] | An object or item used in a dance to complement or extend the choreography.[^11] |
| [[Proprioception]] | Internal sensations and awareness of body position and movement.[^23] |
| [[Regulate]] | To control something, especially by making it work in a particular way.[^4] |
| [[Roles]] | The characteristics and expected social behavior of an individual in a given position (e.g., mother, employer).[^17] |
| [[Self-Determined]] | Determined by oneself.[^14] |
| [[Solo]] | A performance in which the performer has no partner or associate; something undertaken or done alone.[^14] |
| [[Spatial Levels]] | The altitude of a movement or shape (high, middle, low) in relationship to its distance from the floor.[^29] |
| [[Spatial Pathways]] | Patterns of dance movement in and through space (straight, zigzag, curvy, wavy).[^29] |
| [[Spatial Relationship]] | Relationships between dancers or between dancers and objects (beside, in front of, over, through, around, near, far).[^11] |
| [[Spontaneous Movement]] | See *Improvisation*. |
| [[Stamina]] | Great physical or mental strength that allows you to continue doing something for a long time.[^8] |
| [[Symbol]] | An image, sign, or element (such as color) understood by convention or context to suggest some other meaning.[^22] |
| [[Tempo of Movement]] | The pace or speed of a pulse or beat underlying music or movement.[^17] |
| [[Verbal Cues]] | See *Cue*. |
| [[Viewpoint]] | The position from which something is observed.[^17] |
| [[Visual Cues]] | See *Cue*. |

## Music Glossary

> **Note:** These terms are found in addition to those in the General Glossary, in the K-12 General Music as well as Vocal and Instrumental Music Standard Courses of Study.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| [[Accidentals]] | A note of a pitch (or pitch class) not a member of the scale or mode indicated by the key signature, including the sharp (♯), flat (♭), and natural (♮) symbols.[^29] |
| [[Active Listening]] | Enables students to experience music appreciation from three perspectives: 1) enjoyment, relaxation, or inspiration; 2) analytical listening; 3) evaluative listening.[^24] |
| [[Alla Breve]] | A tempo marking indicating a quick duple meter with the half-note getting the beat; sometimes referred to as cut time.[^31] |
| [[Arrangement]] | Resetting music for other instruments or voices or for another style of performance than originally written.[^19] |
| [[Articulations]] | The characteristics of attack and decay of tones and the manner in which tones in sequence are connected or disconnected.[^19] |
| [[Audiate]] | Hear and comprehend sounds in one's head (inner hearing), even when no sound is present.[^17] |
| [[Aural]] | Relating to the sense of hearing, listening.[^24] |
| [[Balance]] | The state of equilibrium where all the component parts of the music create a unified whole.[^19] |
| [[Blues Scales]] | A major scale in which the third and seventh are lowered a half-step.[^31] |
| [[Canons]] | A musical form in which a melody is imitated exactly in one or more parts (similar to a round).[^24] |
| [[Chromatic]] | Scope of the instrument or composition in half steps.[^29] |
| [[Classify]] | Grouping entities based on their common relationships.[^19] |
| [[Classroom Instruments]] | Instruments typically used in the general music classroom (recorders, autoharps, mallet instruments, pitched and unpitched percussion, keyboards, electronic instruments).[^19] |
| [[Commercial Marketing]] | The various commercial and informational channels and forums for media artworks (TV, radio, internet, fine arts, non-profit, communications).[^29] |
| [[Compose]] | The act of inventing or creating music.[^19] |
| [[Composition]] | Original piece of music that can be repeated, typically developed over time, and preserved in notation or sound recording.[^17] |
| [[Conducting Patterns and Gestures]] | Indicating the meter, tempo, changes in tempo and dynamics, and other musical outcomes through gestures of the hands and arms.[^19] |
| [[Countermelody]] | An alternate melody sung along with and as a companion to the main melody.[^19] |
| [[Dynamics]] | Level or range of loudness of a sound or sounds.[^17] |
| [[Elements of Music]] | Pitch (melody), rhythm, harmony, dynamics, timbre (tone color), texture, form, and tempo.[^19] |
| [[Ensembles]] | Groups of individuals organized to perform artistic work (bands, orchestras, choirs, duets, trios, quartets, etc.).[^17] |
| [[Form]] | The overall structural organization of a music composition (AB, ABA, Call and Response, Rondo, Theme and Variations, Sonata-allegro, etc.).[^19] |
| [[Harmony / Harmonization]] | Chordal structure of a music composition; the process of applying stylistically appropriate harmony (chords, countermelodies, ostinato) to melodic material.[^17] |
| [[Hemiola]] | A rhythmic pattern of syncopated beats.[^29] |
| [[Homophonic]] | Musical texture in which all parts move in the same rhythm but use different pitches (hymn style); also, a melody supported by chords.[^17] |
| [[Iconic Notation]] | Graphic representations indicating pitch or rhythm, used in lieu of or alongside traditional symbols.[^19] |
| [[Interval]] | The relationship among pitches (e.g., C4 and E4 produce a major third).[^19] |
| [[Key]] | The sharps or flats placed at the beginning of a composition denoting the scale on which the music is based.[^19] |
| [[Major]] | A scale built on the sequence: whole-step, whole-step, half-step, whole-step, whole-step, whole-step, half-step.[^19] |
| [[Melodic Pattern]] | Grouping, generally brief, of tones or pitches.[^17] |
| [[Melodic Phrase]] | Short section or series of notes within a larger work that constitutes a single coherent melodic idea.[^17] |
| [[Melody]] | Linear succession of sounds (pitches) and silences moving through time; the horizontal structure of music.[^17] |
| [[Meter]] | The grouping of rhythmic pulses or beats, organized and indicated by a meter signature.[^19] |
| [[Minor]] | A scale built on the sequence: whole-step, half-step, whole-step, whole-step, half-step, whole-step, whole-step.[^19] |
| [[Monophonic]] | Musical texture consisting of a single, unaccompanied melodic line.[^17] |
| [[Musical Phrase]] | Dividing musical sentences or thoughts into melodic and/or rhythmic sections, similar to punctuation in language.[^19] |
| [[Notate]] | Use of symbols to indicate pitch, rhythm, and expressive elements of a composition.[^19] |
| [[Ostinato]] | A continually recurring rhythmic or melodic pattern.[^19] |
| [[Partner Songs]] | Two or more songs that can be sung at the same time to create harmony.[^19] |
| [[Pentatonic Scale]] | A scale consisting of five pitches; often omits the fourth and seventh (or second and sixth) degrees of a major scale.[^19] |
| [[Phrasing]] | Performance of a musical phrase using expressive qualities (dynamics, tempo, articulation, timbre) to convey a thought, mood, or feeling.[^17] |
| [[Pitch]] | The highness or lowness of a tone, determined by frequency of vibration.[^19] |
| [[Polyphonic]] | Musical texture in which two or more melodies sound simultaneously.[^17] |
| [[Program]] | Presentation of a sequence of musical works performed by individual musicians or groups in a concert, recital, or other setting.[^17] |
| [[Repertoire]] | A collection of music that a student has learned and is prepared to demonstrate; classified into difficulty levels (Novice, Developing, Intermediate, Accomplished, Advanced).[^19] |
| [[Rhythm]] | Duration or length of sounds and silences that occur in music; organization of sounds and silences in time.[^17] |
| [[Rhythmic Pattern]] | Grouping, generally brief, of long and short sounds and silences.[^17] |
| [[Rhythmic Phrase]] | Short section or series of notes within a larger work that constitutes a single coherent rhythmic idea.[^17] |
| [[Rounds]] | A composition for two or more voices in which one voice enters after another in exact imitation of the first.[^19] |
| [[Scales]] | A sequence of tones, usually within an octave, used as the basis of a composition.[^19] |
| [[Sight-Read]] | The reading (singing or playing) of music at first sight.[^19] |
| [[Soundscapes]] | Individual sounds (or sound masses) and silences whose succession forms patterns and contrasting units that are perceived as musical.[^17] |
| [[Standard Notation System]] | System for visually representing musical sound (staff notation, tablature, lead-sheet notation).[^17] |
| [[Syncopation]] | Deliberate upsetting of the meter or pulse by temporarily shifting the accent to a weak beat or off-beat.[^29] |
| [[Tempo]] | The speed of music.[^19] |
| [[Tension and Release]] | Musical device (stress, instability, intensity) followed by relaxation, stability, or resolution used to create a flow of feeling.[^17] |
| [[Texture]] | Manner in which the harmonic (vertical) and melodic (horizontal) elements are combined to create layers of sound.[^17] |
| [[Timbre]] | The character or quality of a sound that distinguishes one instrument, voice, or other sound source from another.[^19] |
| [[Tonality]] | The tonal center (key) around which a piece of music is centered.[^17] |
| [[Triplets]] | Three notes of equal length performed in the duration of two notes of equal length.[^29] |
| [[Unison]] | Singing or playing the same notes by all singers or players, either at exactly the same pitch or in a different octave.[^19] |
| [[Unity]] | Presence of structural coherence within a work, generally achieved through the repetition of various elements of music.[^17] |
| [[Variety]] | Presence of structural contrast within a work for the purpose of creating and sustaining interest.[^17] |

## Theatre Glossary

> **Note:** These terms are found in addition to those in the General Glossary, in the K-12 Theatre Arts as well as Technical Theatre Standard Courses of Study.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| [[Actor]] | The people in a play or dramatic work who portray the characters (through thought, action, dialogue, costumes, and makeup).[^29] |
| [[Aesthetics]] | Artistic choices which give meaning and clarity to a production.[^29] |
| [[Blocking]] | Where the actors move on stage.[^21] |
| [[Characterization]] | The process of exploring the physical, social, and psychological aspects of a role in order to create a believable character.[^8] |
| [[Characters]] | A person portrayed in a drama, novel, or other artistic piece.[^21] |
| [[Conflict]] | The problem, confrontation, or struggle in a scene or play (character vs. self, character, nature, society, or supernatural).[^17] |
| [[Costume]] | Clothing worn by actors to portray specific characters.[^29] |
| [[Designer]] | Responsible for creating the look and feel of a production (sets, lighting, sound, costume, properties, makeup).[^29] |
| [[Devised Theatre]] | Creation of an original performance piece by an ensemble.[^17] |
| [[Dialogue]] | A conversation between two or more characters.[^17] |
| [[Director]] | The person who oversees the entire process of staging a production, interpreting the text, and conveying a central concept or theme.[^29] |
| [[Dramatic Elements]] | See *Elements*. |
| [[Dramatic Play]] | Spontaneous dramatic enactment often done by children pretending or imitating while playing.[^21] |
| [[Dramatic Presentation]] | The production of a play or dramatic work, or the specific acting/technical job of a production member.[^29] |
| [[Dramatic Structure]] | The organization of a script or storyline (exposition, rising action, climax, falling action).[^21] |
| [[Elements (Technical / Dramatic / Theatrical)]] | Production Elements: sets, sound, costumes, lights, music, props, makeup. Technical Elements: set, lighting, sound, costumes, properties, hair and makeup. Story Elements: characters, setting, plot, conflict, resolution.[^29] |
| [[Embodiment]] | To physicalize a movement, concept, or idea through the body.[^17] |
| [[Environment]] | Physical surroundings that establish place, time, and atmosphere/mood; conditions that affect the emotions, thoughts, and actions of characters.[^21] |
| [[Imitate]] | To copy or mimic the actions, appearance, mannerisms, or speech of others.[^21] |
| [[Lighting]] | The illumination of the performance dictated by circumstances (source of light, time of day, mood, scenic design requirements).[^29] |
| [[Monologue]] | A speech made by a single character (often delivered to another character or the audience).[^29] |
| [[Pantomime]] | A situation where a performer relies totally on gesture, facial expression, and movement rather than speech.[^21] |
| [[Performance Spaces]] | Areas that make up the theater and performing space (apron, borders, grand drape, wing space, etc.).[^29] |
| [[Playwright]] | The person responsible for writing plays for performance in the theatre.[^29] |
| [[Plot]] | A narrative revealed through action and/or dialogue; includes exposition, inciting incident, conflict, rising action, climax, and resolution or falling action.[^17] |
| [[Production Process]] | The team of people on-stage and behind the scenes that make a performance possible (actors, director, technical crew, producer).[^29] |
| [[Prop]] | Anything the actor uses on stage that is not scenery (e.g., a coffee cup, cell phone).[^29] |
| [[Scenery]] | The scenery constructed for a theatrical performance.[^21] |
| [[Setting]] | The locale, period, and time in which the action of a play takes place.[^29] |
| [[Situation]] | A combination of circumstances at a given moment.[^21] |
| [[Stagecraft]] | The technical aspect of theatrical, film, and video production (set construction, lighting, sound, costumes, makeup, props, stage management).[^29] |
| [[Staging]] | Patterns of movement in a scene or play (stage crosses, entrances, exits) that help convey meaning.[^17] |
| [[Staging Conventions]] | Practices or devices that the audience and actors accept (narrator, flashback, aside).[^17] |
| [[Story Elements]] | Characters, setting, plot, conflict, and resolution; dramatic structure.[^29] |
| [[Technical Elements]] | See *Elements*. |
| [[Technical Theatre]] | The elements of spectacle (sets, sound, costume, lights, music, props, makeup) used to create a unified and meaningful design for a theatrical production.[^17] |

## Visual Arts Glossary

> **Note:** These terms are found in addition to those in the General Glossary, in the K-12 Visual Arts Standard Course of Study.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| [[Abstract Art]] | Art derived from realism but deviating in appearance; maintaining the essentials of shape, line, color, and texture relating to the subject.[^2] |
| [[Aesthetic]] | The study or theory of the beautiful, in taste or art.[^22] |
| [[Art Exhibitions]] | A public showing of works of art, objects of manufacture, or athletic skill.[^14] |
| [[Artist Statement]] | Information about context, explanations of process, descriptions of learning, related stories, reflections, or other details shared by the artist to extend and deepen understanding of their artwork.[^17] |
| [[Body of Work / Art]] | Aggregate; quantity of an artist's output.[^14] |
| [[Ceramics]] | Functional and decorative objects made from clay.[^22] |
| [[Cityscapes]] | An artistic representation of a city.[^14] |
| [[Collection]] | An accumulation of objects gathered for study, comparison, or exhibition, or as a hobby.[^14] |
| [[Commercial Art]] | Art applied to commercial purposes.[^14] |
| [[Composition]] | The organization of the elements of art and principles of design in creating a work of art.[^2] |
| [[Copying]] | An imitation, transcript, or reproduction of an original work (painting, letter, dress).[^14] |
| [[Creative Design Process]] | Logical progression in making art: choose topic, research, identify important aspects, devise problems, ask questions, solve problems, produce material, design artwork, self-evaluate, revise, use feedback.[^12] |
| [[Curate]] | Collect, sort, and organize objects, artworks, and artifacts; preserve and maintain historical records and catalogue exhibits.[^2] |
| [[Derivative]] | Having parts that originate from another source.[^14] |
| [[Digital Media (Format)]] | Anything in electronic form (photos, images, video, audio files, or artwork) created or presented through electronic means.[^17] |
| [[Drawing]] | The art or technique of representing an object or outlining a figure, plan, or sketch by means of lines.[^14] |
| [[Elements of Art]] | The "visual tools" artists use to create works of art: form, shape, line, texture, color, space, and value.[^2] |
| [[Fibers]] | Thread-like parts that form plant or artificial material and can be made into cloth.[^4] |
| [[Formative]] | A process used by teachers and students during instruction that provides feedback to adjust ongoing teaching and learning.[^28] |
| [[Forms]] | A shape having three dimensions—height, width, and depth.[^2] |
| [[Imaginary Sources of Information]] | A mental picture of something that is unlike things one has seen.[^22] |
| [[Imaginative Play]] | Experimentation by children in defining identities and points of view by developing skills in conceiving, planning, making art, and communicating.[^17] |
| [[Landscapes]] | A picture representing a view of natural inland scenery.[^14] |
| [[Materials]] | Substances out of which art is made or composed, ranging from traditional to "non-art" material and virtual, cybernetic, and simulated materials.[^17] |
| [[Media / Medium]] | Modes of artistic expression or communication; material or other resources used for creating art.[^17] |
| [[Movements]] | The path the viewer's eye takes through the work of art; the arrangement of elements organized to create a sense of motion.[^2] |
| [[Non-objective Art]] | Representing or intending to represent no natural or actual object, figure, or scene.[^14] |
| [[Painting / Paint]] | Producing lines and colors on a surface by applying pigments.[^14] |
| [[Periods]] | Of, relating to, or representing a particular historical period.[^14] |
| [[Personal Identity]] | Distinguishing character of an individual; includes physical, psychological, and interpersonal characteristics, as well as affiliations (ethnicity, social roles).[^20] |
| [[Portfolio]] | An actual or virtual collection of artworks and documentation demonstrating art and design knowledge and skills, organized to reflect an individual's creative growth and artistic literacy.[^17] |
| [[Portraits]] | Artwork that shows the face or figure of a person.[^22] |
| [[Principles of Design]] | The way elements of art (balance, movement, pattern/repetition, unity/variety, emphasis) are used to create a composition.[^22] |
| [[Printmaking]] | The art of using a printing plate or stamp to create one or a series of multiple originals called prints.[^22] |
| [[Qualities]] | The characteristics, constraints, and limitations of a particular medium (e.g., canvas texture, color, brush texture).[^25] |
| [[Sculpture]] | Carved, cut, hewn, cast, molded, welded, or assembled three-dimensional representations, forms, or figures.[^22] |
| [[Seascapes]] | A picture representing a scene at sea.[^14] |
| [[Still Life]] | An arrangement of inanimate objects.[^22] |
| [[Summative]] | The effort to summarize student learning at a particular point in time (end of chapter, unit, grading period, semester, year, or course).[^28] |
| [[Symbol / Symbolism]] | An image, sign, or element (e.g., color) understood by convention or context to suggest another meaning.[^22] |
| [[Tracing]] | To copy (something, such as a drawing) by following the lines or letters as seen through a transparent superimposed sheet.[^14] |
| [[Viewpoints]] | The position from which something or someone is observed.[^17] |
| [[Visual Arts]] | As defined by the National Art Education Association: traditional fine arts (drawing, painting, printmaking, photography, sculpture), media arts (animation, video, digital technologies), design (communication, product, interactive), environmental arts (architecture, landscape architecture, interior/urban planning), and conceptual, performance, participatory, street, and folk arts.[^17] |
